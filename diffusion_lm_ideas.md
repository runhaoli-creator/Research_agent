## Idea 1: Learned Unmasking Policy — RL for Optimal Token Revelation Order

**Core insight:** LogicDiff (2025) improved LLaDA-8B from 22.0% to 60.7% on GSM8K WITHOUT any training — just by changing the order tokens are unmasked. This proves unmasking order is enormously important. But LogicDiff uses a fixed heuristic (unmask "easy" tokens first based on model confidence). We propose LEARNING the optimal unmasking policy via RL, treating the denoising process as a sequential decision problem where the agent chooses WHICH tokens to reveal at each step.

**Why this could be oral:** The LogicDiff result is shocking — a 3x improvement from ordering alone. Learning the optimal order (rather than using a heuristic) should be strictly better. This reframes diffusion decoding as a planning problem, connecting two major research areas. The contribution is both algorithmic (learned unmasking policy) and conceptual (diffusion as sequential decision-making).

**Closest work:** LogicDiff uses confidence-based heuristic ordering. MDLM/LLaDA use random or log-linear schedules. No paper learns the unmasking policy via RL.

**Technical mechanism:** Model the unmasking process as an MDP: state = current partially-masked sequence, action = which positions to unmask next (top-k from a policy network), reward = final output quality (verifiable for math, LLM-judge for open-ended). Train a lightweight policy network (2-layer MLP on the dLLM's hidden states) via REINFORCE or GRPO to select the optimal unmask set at each step. The policy learns: unmask structural tokens (punctuation, common words) early, content-bearing tokens (numbers, key nouns, logical connectives) later when they have more context.

**Experiments:** Train on LLaDA-8B or Dream-7B. Evaluate on GSM8K, MATH, AIME (reasoning), HumanEval (code), MT-Bench (open-ended). Compare: random unmasking, confidence-based (LogicDiff), entropy-based, learned policy (ours). Ablate: policy network size, number of unmasking steps, top-k per step, reward type.

## Idea 2: DPO for Diffusion Language Models via Denoising Trajectory Preferences

**Core insight:** Preference optimization (DPO/RLHF) is what made ChatGPT work. But it's essentially impossible for current dLLMs because you can't compute p(x) for a complete sequence — you only have an ELBO lower bound. LLaDA2.0 mentions DPO but provides no details or results. We propose Denoising Trajectory DPO (DT-DPO): instead of computing preferences over final outputs (which requires intractable p(x)), compute preferences over DENOISING TRAJECTORIES where step-level probabilities ARE tractable.

**Why this could be oral:** Alignment is the single biggest missing capability for dLLMs. Every AR model gets DPO/RLHF. dLLMs have nothing. Solving this opens dLLMs to all instruction-following, safety, and preference applications. The denoising trajectory formulation is a clean theoretical contribution.

**Closest work:** d1/JustGRPO/StableDRL do RL for math with verifiable rewards. No paper does DPO/RLHF for open-ended preference alignment of dLLMs. LLaDA2.0 claims DPO but no details.

**Technical mechanism:** (1) Generate pairs of denoising trajectories (τ_w, τ_l) — one preferred, one dispreferred — from a prompt. (2) Each trajectory is a sequence of partially-masked states: τ = (x_T, x_{T-1}, ..., x_0). (3) The probability of a trajectory IS tractable: p(τ) = ∏_t p(x_{t-1} | x_t). (4) DPO objective: maximize log σ(β * (log p(τ_w) - log p(τ_l))) where p(τ) is the trajectory probability, not the intractable sequence probability p(x_0). (5) Preference data: use GPT-4 to rank output pairs from the base dLLM, then compute their denoising trajectories via the forward diffusion process.

**Experiments:** Train on LLaDA-8B-Instruct. Evaluate on AlpacaEval 2.0, MT-Bench, Arena-Hard. Compare: base dLLM, SFT-only dLLM, AR DPO (Llama-3.1-8B-DPO as reference), GRPO-only dLLM, our DT-DPO. Ablate: β, number of preference pairs (1K, 5K, 20K, 100K), trajectory length, preference source (GPT-4 vs. human vs. self-judge).

## Idea 3: Denoising as Deliberate Reasoning — Using Diffusion Steps as Thinking Steps

**Core insight:** AR models "think" by generating chain-of-thought tokens sequentially. dLLMs generate everything at once — they have no mechanism for iterative reasoning. We propose using the denoising steps themselves as "thinking steps": instead of each step merely unmasking tokens, each step REVISES the entire draft, progressively improving reasoning quality. The number of denoising steps becomes the "thinking budget" — more steps = deeper reasoning.

**Why this could be oral:** This redefines what denoising means for text. Currently, denoising just fills in blanks. We make denoising a reasoning process where the model iteratively refines its answer — semantically, not just lexically. This is the diffusion analog of "thinking tokens" and could enable a new paradigm of "parallel thinking" where all reasoning steps are refined simultaneously.

**Closest work:** Coconut (Meta, 2412.06769) does latent reasoning for AR models. No paper treats the diffusion denoising trajectory as iterative reasoning refinement. Dream 7B's CART rescheduling adjusts noise but doesn't frame denoising as reasoning.

**Technical mechanism:** Modify the training objective: instead of training to predict the clean text x_0 from noisy x_t in one shot (standard), train the model to predict a BETTER version x_{t'} where t' < t (not necessarily x_0). This means each denoising step produces a version that's better but not final. The model learns to use intermediate steps for reasoning: step 1 might set up the problem structure, step 5 fills in key relationships, step 10 computes the answer. Train with a "progressive improvement" loss that rewards each step for improving over the previous step (measured by proximity to x_0). At inference, the model reasons by iterating: each step refines the CONTENT (not just unmasking) of the answer.

**Experiments:** Train on LLaDA-8B architecture. Evaluate on GSM8K, MATH, GPQA, ARC-Challenge. Key experiment: vary number of denoising steps at inference (5, 10, 20, 50, 100) and plot accuracy. If denoising=reasoning, more steps should improve accuracy (analogous to more thinking tokens for AR). Compare: standard LLaDA, Dream-7B, AR Llama-3.1-8B with CoT, our method. Ablate: progressive improvement loss weight, number of training steps, step prediction target (x_0 vs. x_{t'}).

## Idea 4: Self-Refining Diffusion — Re-Corruption and Re-Denoising for Quality Improvement

**Core insight:** dLLMs cannot self-correct — once tokens are unmasked, they're fixed. We propose a self-refinement mechanism unique to diffusion: after generating output x_0, RE-CORRUPT it (add noise to get x_t for some t < T), then RE-DENOISE with additional conditioning signals (self-evaluation, quality score, specific critique). Each cycle of corruption→denoising improves the output. This is the text analog of SDEdit (image editing via noise injection + denoising).

**Why this could be oral:** Self-correction is a key capability AR models also struggle with. For dLLMs, the diffusion framework provides a NATURAL mechanism for iterative refinement that AR models lack — you can literally "add noise and denoise again" with new conditioning. This is elegant, simple, and connects to the rich literature on iterative refinement in image diffusion.

**Closest work:** SDEdit (2021) does this for images. No paper does re-corruption → re-denoising for text quality improvement. DreamOn does variable-length infilling but not quality-based re-refinement. AR self-correction papers (Reflexion, SCoRe) use separate correction steps, not the generation mechanism itself.

**Technical mechanism:** (1) Generate x_0 from the dLLM normally. (2) Self-evaluate: use the same dLLM or a small critic to identify quality issues (correctness, coherence, style). (3) Re-corrupt: add noise back to x_0 to get x_t, with t proportional to how much refinement is needed. More corruption = more freedom to change. (4) Re-denoise: denoise x_t → x_0' with the quality critique as additional conditioning (prepended to the prompt or injected via cross-attention). (5) Repeat for k rounds. The key: the noise level t controls the refinement granularity — low t for minor fixes (word choice, grammar), high t for major restructuring (logic, argument flow).

**Experiments:** Train on LLaDA-8B with an additional "critique-conditioned denoising" training phase. Evaluate on MT-Bench, AlpacaEval, GSM8K (self-correction), HumanEval (code debugging). Compare: base dLLM, base + re-denoise without critique, base + re-denoise with critique (ours), AR self-correction (Reflexion, SCoRe), AR self-consistency. Ablate: noise level t, number of refinement rounds k, critique source (self vs. external LLM), which tokens to re-corrupt (all vs. low-confidence vs. critiqued spans).

## Idea 5: Grammar-Constrained Diffusion Decoding via Energy Guidance

**Core insight:** AR models can enforce output structure (JSON, XML, SQL, code with type systems) via constrained decoding — rejecting invalid tokens at each step. dLLMs generate all tokens in parallel with no token-by-token constraint mechanism. We propose formulating structural constraints as differentiable energy functions and using them to guide the diffusion denoising process — analogous to classifier guidance in image diffusion.

**Why this could be oral:** Structured output is essential for every production application (API calls, code, data extraction). dLLMs currently CANNOT guarantee valid structure. This is the single biggest practical barrier to dLLM adoption. Solving it would immediately make dLLMs production-ready for tool use, function calling, and code generation.

**Closest work:** S3 (2507.04504) uses dLLM reverse reasoning for JSON but doesn't guarantee validity. Classifier-free guidance for masked diffusion (2507.08965) adjusts generation toward a condition but doesn't enforce hard constraints. AR constrained decoding (Outlines, Guidance) is token-sequential and doesn't apply.

**Technical mechanism:** (1) Define a constraint energy function E(x) that is low when x satisfies the grammar and high when it doesn't. For JSON: E = number of syntax violations (unclosed brackets, invalid types). For CFG: E = minimum edit distance to the nearest valid parse. (2) Make E differentiable via relaxation: during denoising, work with soft token probabilities (not hard tokens), compute E on the soft distribution, backpropagate through E to get gradients on the token logits. (3) At each denoising step, modify the logits: logits' = logits - λ * ∇_{logits} E(softmax(logits)). This pushes the generation toward valid structures. (4) For hard guarantees: after the final denoising step, project onto the valid set via a fast constraint solver (for JSON: fix syntax errors deterministically).

**Experiments:** Train/evaluate on LLaDA-8B-Instruct. Tasks: (1) JSON generation from natural language (ToolBench, 1000 API schemas), (2) SQL generation (Spider, 1034 databases), (3) Python code with type constraints (HumanEval + mypy type checking), (4) Markdown with structure constraints. Compare: base dLLM (no constraints), base + post-hoc fixing, base + rejection sampling, AR constrained decoding (Outlines on Llama-3.1-8B), our energy-guided decoding. Metrics: validity rate (% outputs that parse), quality (correctness), speed. Ablate: guidance strength λ, energy function design, number of denoising steps, constraint complexity.

# Implementation Prompts

## Idea 1: Learned Unmasking Policy

Build a project that learns the optimal token unmasking order for masked diffusion language models via RL, inspired by LogicDiff's finding that changing unmasking order alone improved LLaDA-8B from 22.0% to 60.7% on GSM8K without any training. This idea is verified novel — LogicDiff uses a confidence-based heuristic, MDLM and LLaDA use random or log-linear schedules, and no paper learns the unmasking policy via RL. Use LLaDA-8B-Instruct (arXiv 2502.09992) as the base diffusion language model, loading from HuggingFace GSAI-ML/LLaDA-8B-Instruct. Model the unmasking process as an MDP: state is the current partially-masked sequence with its hidden state representations, action is selecting which K positions to unmask next (K is a hyperparameter, try 1, 5, 10, 20), transition is the dLLM filling in those positions, and reward is the final output quality measured by math correctness for verifiable tasks or LLM-judge score for open-ended tasks. The policy network is a lightweight 2-layer MLP (hidden_dim to 512 to num_positions with softmax) that takes the dLLM's last-layer hidden states at each masked position and outputs a score for how valuable it is to unmask that position next. Train the policy via REINFORCE with baseline (the baseline is the average reward of random unmasking) or GRPO if generating multiple unmasking trajectories per prompt. The total number of denoising steps is fixed (try 10, 20, 50) and at each step the policy selects the top-K positions to unmask. Train on MATH training set (7.5K problems) and GSM8K training set (7.5K problems) where reward is binary correctness. Evaluate on 6 datasets: GSM8K test (1319 problems), MATH-500, AIME 2024 (30 problems), GPQA Diamond (198 problems), HumanEval (164 problems), and MT-Bench (80 prompts using LLM-judge score). Compare against 7 baselines: (1) random unmasking schedule (standard LLaDA default), (2) log-linear schedule, (3) confidence-based unmasking (LogicDiff — unmask highest-confidence positions first), (4) entropy-based unmasking (unmask lowest-entropy positions first), (5) reverse-confidence (unmask hardest positions first, opposite of LogicDiff), (6) AR Llama-3.1-8B with CoT as the autoregressive reference, and (7) Dream-7B with CART rescheduling. Run 8 ablations: (1) K values per step at 1, 5, 10, 20 positions, (2) total denoising steps at 10, 20, 50, 100, (3) policy network size 256 versus 512 versus 1024 hidden, (4) training reward type (binary correctness versus partial credit versus LLM-judge), (5) RL algorithm REINFORCE versus GRPO, (6) train on math only versus math plus code, (7) with versus without the base dLLM's confidence as input feature to the policy, and (8) curriculum from easy to hard problems. Analysis: visualize the learned unmasking order on example problems showing what the policy learns to unmask first (expect: structural tokens and operators early, numbers and variable names late), plot accuracy versus number of denoising steps showing the scaling behavior, and compare the learned order to LogicDiff's heuristic showing where they agree and disagree. Key result: learned policy should outperform LogicDiff's heuristic by 10-20% on GSM8K because it adapts the order per-problem rather than using a fixed heuristic. Pitfalls: reward sparsity in RL (fix with partial credit rewards), policy overfitting to training distribution (fix with diverse training prompts), and computational overhead of running the policy at each step (fix by showing the MLP adds <1% overhead to total inference time).

## Idea 2: DPO for Diffusion Language Models

Build a project that adapts Direct Preference Optimization to diffusion language models by defining preferences over denoising trajectories rather than intractable sequence probabilities. This idea is verified novel — d1 and JustGRPO do RL with verifiable rewards only, StableDRL fixes GRPO stability but still needs verifiable rewards, LLaDA2.0 mentions DPO but provides no details or results, and no paper demonstrates open-ended preference alignment for dLLMs. The core insight is that while p(x_0) is intractable for a dLLM, the trajectory probability p(τ) = ∏_t p(x_{t-1} | x_t) IS tractable because each step's conditional probability can be computed. Use LLaDA-8B-Instruct as the base model. Step 1: generate 50K prompt-response pairs from the base dLLM on UltraFeedback prompts (60K diverse instructions). Step 2: for each prompt, generate 4 responses and have GPT-4 rank them pairwise, producing approximately 100K preference pairs (chosen, rejected). Step 3: for each response, compute its denoising trajectory by running the forward diffusion process (corruption) from x_0 to x_T, recording intermediate states. Step 4: compute trajectory log-probabilities: log p(τ) = sum_t log p(x_{t-1} | x_t, prompt) where each conditional is the dLLM's output probability at that denoising step. Step 5: DT-DPO loss = -log σ(β * (log p_θ(τ_w) / p_ref(τ_w) - log p_θ(τ_l) / p_ref(τ_l))) where τ_w and τ_l are the winning and losing trajectories and p_ref is the reference (base) model's trajectory probability. Train with AdamW, learning rate 5e-7, β values in 0.05, 0.1, 0.2, batch size 32, for 3 epochs. Evaluate on 5 benchmarks: AlpacaEval 2.0 (length-controlled win rate against GPT-4), MT-Bench (multi-turn, GPT-4 judge), Arena-Hard (500 hard prompts), IFEval (instruction following, 541 prompts), and MATH-500 (to check reasoning preservation). Compare against 6 baselines: (1) base LLaDA-8B-Instruct (SFT only), (2) LLaDA + GRPO with verifiable math rewards (d1-style), (3) LLaDA + StableDRL, (4) AR baseline Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct-DPO with standard DPO, (5) LLaDA + rejection sampling fine-tuning (best-of-N then SFT), and (6) LLaDA + RLHF with a separate reward model. Run 8 ablations: (1) β values at 0.01, 0.05, 0.1, 0.2, 0.5, (2) number of preference pairs at 1K, 5K, 20K, 50K, 100K, (3) number of denoising steps used for trajectory computation at 10, 20, 50, (4) preference source GPT-4 versus Claude versus self-judge, (5) trajectory computation method (forward corruption versus reverse denoising), (6) with versus without KL regularization, (7) training epochs 1, 2, 3, 5, and (8) combining DT-DPO with math GRPO (sequential versus interleaved). Analysis: plot AlpacaEval win rate over training showing DT-DPO improvement curve, compare trajectory-level DPO versus output-level DPO (using ELBO as proxy for p(x_0)) to show trajectory formulation is essential, measure KL divergence from reference to quantify how much the model changes, and check whether DPO degrades reasoning (MATH-500 before/after). Pitfalls: trajectory computation is expensive because you need T forward passes per response (fix by using fewer trajectory steps, 10-20 instead of full T), preference annotation quality (fix with GPT-4 + human validation on 500 examples), and the model might learn surface preferences like length (fix with length-controlled evaluation on AlpacaEval).

## Idea 3: Denoising as Deliberate Reasoning

Build a project that treats the diffusion denoising process as iterative reasoning refinement, where each denoising step doesn't just unmask tokens but REVISES the entire draft to progressively improve reasoning quality. This idea is verified novel — no paper frames denoising as reasoning refinement for text, Coconut (arXiv 2412.06769) does latent iterative reasoning for AR models but not for diffusion, and Dream 7B's CART adjusts noise schedules but doesn't conceptualize steps as reasoning stages. The core insight: if each denoising step produces a progressively BETTER answer (not just a less noisy one), then the number of denoising steps becomes a "thinking budget" analogous to chain-of-thought length. Use LLaDA-8B architecture (same as arXiv 2502.09992) modified so that the model is trained to predict a BETTER intermediate version x_{t'} from x_t (where t' < t) rather than always predicting x_0 directly. Implement this by changing the training target: standard MDLM trains with loss = CE(model(x_t), x_0) at every step. Our progressive refinement loss uses a mixture: loss = α * CE(model(x_t), x_{t-Δ}) + (1-α) * CE(model(x_t), x_0) where x_{t-Δ} is a partially-denoised version that is better than x_t but not yet x_0, and α decays over training from 0.8 to 0.2 (curriculum from easy refinement to full prediction). At inference, run more denoising steps than standard (50-100 versus 10-20), and each step refines the CONTENT not just unmasks tokens. Train on Llama-3.1-8B converted to masked diffusion (following LLaDA2.0's conversion recipe) on 50B tokens from SlimPajama plus 5B tokens of math and code. Evaluate on 6 datasets: GSM8K, MATH-500, AIME 2024, GPQA Diamond, ARC-Challenge, HumanEval. The KEY experiment: plot accuracy versus number of denoising steps at inference (5, 10, 20, 50, 100, 200) for both standard LLaDA and our method — if denoising=reasoning, our method should show monotonic improvement with more steps while standard LLaDA plateaus or degrades. Compare against 7 baselines: (1) standard LLaDA-8B with default schedule, (2) Dream-7B with CART, (3) LLaDA with LogicDiff unmasking, (4) AR Llama-3.1-8B with CoT (varying CoT length), (5) AR Llama-3.1-8B with best-of-N (varying N), (6) Coconut latent reasoning (if reproducible), and (7) our method without progressive loss (trained with standard x_0 prediction but evaluated with many steps). Run 8 ablations: (1) α schedule (constant versus decaying versus step-function), (2) Δ values for the intermediate target (Δ=1 versus 5 versus 10), (3) number of training denoising steps, (4) inference steps (5 to 200, the scaling curve), (5) with versus without AR warm-start, (6) progressive loss weight at 0.2, 0.5, 0.8, (7) noise type masked versus uniform, and (8) model scale 3B versus 8B versus 14B. Analysis: the accuracy-versus-steps scaling curve is Figure 1 and the paper's centerpiece — it must show clear monotonic improvement for our method where baselines plateau. Visualize intermediate denoising steps on example math problems showing how the reasoning structure evolves from step 5 to step 50 (expect early steps to set up equations, later steps to compute answers). Measure "revision rate" — what fraction of tokens change between consecutive steps, showing the model is genuinely revising not just unmasking. Pitfalls: the model might learn to ignore the progressive loss and just predict x_0 at every step (fix by monitoring revision rate during training and increasing α if revision rate drops), training instability from the mixed loss (fix with gradient clipping and careful α scheduling), and the method might not help on tasks that don't require multi-step reasoning (expected — it should shine on hard reasoning, be neutral on easy generation).

## Idea 4: Self-Refining Diffusion via Re-Corruption

Build a project implementing self-refinement for diffusion language models via re-corruption and conditioned re-denoising, analogous to SDEdit for images but adapted to discrete text. This idea is verified novel — SDEdit (arXiv 2108.01073) does noise-then-denoise for image editing, no paper applies this to text quality improvement, DreamOn does variable-length infilling not quality refinement, and AR self-correction methods (Reflexion, SCoRe) use separate correction steps rather than the generation mechanism itself. The core insight: diffusion models have a UNIQUE self-refinement mechanism unavailable to AR models — you can add noise back to an output and denoise again with new conditioning, and the noise level controls refinement granularity. Use LLaDA-8B-Instruct as the base model. The method has 4 stages per refinement round: (1) Generate: produce initial output x_0 via standard denoising. (2) Critique: use the same dLLM or a small critic model to evaluate x_0 and produce a text critique (e.g., "Step 3 has an arithmetic error" or "The argument in paragraph 2 is circular"). (3) Re-corrupt: apply forward noise to x_0, masking a fraction t of tokens. The fraction t is proportional to the critique severity — minor issues (word choice) use t=0.1 (mask 10% of tokens), major issues (logical errors) use t=0.5 (mask 50%). Choose WHICH tokens to mask based on the critique: if the critique says "Step 3 is wrong", preferentially mask tokens in Step 3. (4) Re-denoise: denoise the partially-corrupted sequence back to a clean output x_0', with the critique text prepended to the prompt as additional conditioning. This allows the model to specifically address the identified issues while preserving the good parts (uncorrupted tokens stay fixed). Repeat for k=1,2,3 rounds. Train with an additional "critique-conditioned denoising" phase: take 50K (output, critique, improved_output) triples generated using GPT-4, corrupt the improved_output, and train the dLLM to denoise back to the improved version conditioned on the critique. This teaches the model to use critique information during denoising. Evaluate on 6 benchmarks: GSM8K, MATH-500, MT-Bench (quality), AlpacaEval 2.0 (preference), HumanEval (code), and a custom "Correction Benchmark" of 500 problems with known errors where the model must fix them. Compare against 7 baselines: (1) base dLLM single-pass generation, (2) base dLLM with re-denoising but NO critique conditioning, (3) base dLLM with re-denoising and RANDOM re-corruption (not critique-targeted), (4) AR self-correction (Reflexion with 3 rounds), (5) AR SCoRe (arXiv 2409.12917), (6) AR self-consistency k=8, and (7) dLLM with multiple independent generations then best-of-N selection. Run 8 ablations: (1) noise level t at 0.1, 0.2, 0.3, 0.5, 0.8, (2) number of refinement rounds k at 1, 2, 3, 5, (3) critique source (self-critique versus external LLM versus no critique), (4) targeted versus random re-corruption, (5) critique-conditioning method (prepend to prompt versus cross-attention injection), (6) amount of critique-conditioned training data at 10K, 25K, 50K, (7) base model scale 3B versus 8B, and (8) re-corruption on all tokens versus only flagged tokens. Analysis: plot quality improvement over refinement rounds showing diminishing returns, visualize which tokens change in each round (expect: error-containing regions change, correct regions stay fixed), measure the critique-response alignment (does the model actually fix what the critique identifies), and compare computational cost per quality point versus baselines. Pitfalls: the model might ignore the critique during re-denoising (fix with targeted training data and evaluation of critique adherence), re-corruption might destroy good content (fix with selective masking guided by critique), and multiple rounds might not help (monitor quality per round and stop if plateau).

## Idea 5: Grammar-Constrained Diffusion Decoding

Build a project that enables diffusion language models to guarantee structurally valid outputs (JSON, SQL, Python, XML) via energy-guided decoding, solving the single biggest practical barrier to dLLM deployment. This idea is verified novel — S3 (arXiv 2507.04504) uses dLLM reverse reasoning for JSON but cannot guarantee validity, classifier-free guidance for masked diffusion (arXiv 2507.08965) adjusts generation toward a condition but doesn't enforce hard structural constraints, and AR constrained decoding libraries (Outlines, llama.cpp grammars) are inherently sequential and don't apply to parallel generation. The core mechanism: define structural constraints as differentiable energy functions E(x) and use their gradients to guide the diffusion denoising process. Use LLaDA-8B-Instruct as the base model. For JSON constraints: E_json(x) = number of syntax violations computed by attempting to parse softmax(logits) as soft token sequences through a differentiable JSON parser (approximate the parser with a neural network trained to detect JSON errors, or use a hand-coded differentiable relaxation: count unmatched brackets via soft counting, check key-value structure via pattern matching on soft tokens). For CFG constraints: E_cfg(x) = negative log probability of the soft token sequence under the grammar, computed via a differentiable CYK parser operating on token probabilities. For type constraints in Python: E_type(x) = number of type errors from a differentiable approximation of mypy. At each denoising step, compute logits from the dLLM, then modify: logits' = logits - λ * ∇_logits E(softmax(logits)) where λ is the guidance strength. This pushes token probabilities toward valid structures. For hard guarantees after the final step, apply a deterministic projection: fix any remaining syntax errors with a minimal-edit constraint solver. Evaluate on 5 tasks: (1) JSON generation from natural language instructions (ToolBench 1000 API schemas), (2) SQL generation (Spider benchmark 1034 databases), (3) Python function generation with type annotations (HumanEval 164 problems + mypy validation), (4) XML document generation (100 custom schemas), and (5) Markdown with structural constraints (headings, lists, code blocks). Compare against 7 baselines: (1) base dLLM unconstrained, (2) base dLLM + post-hoc syntax fixing, (3) base dLLM + rejection sampling (generate until valid, up to 10 attempts), (4) AR Llama-3.1-8B with Outlines constrained decoding, (5) AR Llama-3.1-8B unconstrained + post-hoc fixing, (6) base dLLM + S3 structured scaffolding, and (7) base dLLM + classifier-free guidance toward structure. Metrics: structural validity rate (% outputs that parse without errors), task correctness (% that are semantically correct given the instruction), generation speed (tokens/sec), and quality (human judgment of naturalness on 1-5 scale). Run 8 ablations: (1) guidance strength λ at 0.1, 0.5, 1.0, 5.0, 10.0, (2) energy function design (neural versus hand-coded), (3) number of denoising steps 10, 20, 50, (4) with versus without final projection step, (5) constraint complexity (simple JSON versus nested JSON versus recursive schemas), (6) combining energy guidance with classifier-free guidance, (7) guidance applied at all steps versus only last 5 steps, and (8) soft versus hard token representations in energy computation. Analysis: plot validity rate versus guidance strength showing the quality-structure tradeoff, compare speed versus validity versus quality in a 3-way Pareto plot against all baselines, show examples of the energy-guided generation process at each step demonstrating how invalid structures get corrected during denoising, and test on adversarially complex schemas that specifically exploit parallel generation weaknesses. Pitfalls: the energy function gradient might vanish for discrete tokens (fix with Gumbel-softmax relaxation and straight-through estimators), guidance might be too strong and hurt text quality (fix with adaptive λ that decreases as validity improves), and the differentiable parser approximation might be inaccurate (fix with ablation comparing neural versus hand-coded energy and with the final projection step as safety net).
